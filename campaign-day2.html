The Introduction

The portrait happens in the person’s genuine environment.
Objects behind them — books, drawings, tools — tell their story before they speak a word.

II Wardrobe & Environment
Main Subject — Wardrobe

He is in his world, not on show. Every piece is chosen, nothing is performed.

  • Shirt Warm linen in sand or warm white. Top two buttons open, collar relaxed but clean.
  • Trousers Dark tailored trousers, cognac leather belt.
  • Shoes Leather loafers in tan. No socks visible.
  • Layer No jacket. This is his environment, not a stage.
Background — The Workspace

The workspace IS the background. No extras inside. If others are in the office, they move out of frame entirely.

  • Behind Shelves of books or rolled drawings, window with soft morning light
  • Tone Everything in soft focus, tonally warm. Nothing bright or distracting.
  • Light Window light from left. Diffuse with white sheet if harsh. Natural only.
  • Expression Do not ask him to smile. Ask him to think about something he is proud of. Capture what returns.
III Portrait Direction
Portrait

The Cinematic Portrait

Head and shoulders to mid-chest. Subject slightly off-centre — left or right third of frame. Space on opposite side for caption. Take 40 shots. The portrait lives in 3 of them.

Camera Notes
  • Lens 85mm — shallow depth of field brings subject forward, softens background
  • Framing Head to mid-chest. Off-centre, left or right third. Space for caption overlay.
  • Light Window from left, natural only. No ring light. No flash.
  • Expression “Think about something you are proud of.” Capture what returns.
  • After 10 minutes of detail shots — hands on drawing board, book spine, window view
Director’s Note

The portrait is the first time the audience sees this person. Light, expression, objects — all must communicate someone worth listening to before they speak.

Tuesday 7:30pm is peak Instagram browsing. Portrait posts on Tuesday outperform any other weekday — the audience is settled but not yet in weekend mode.

Caption Principle

Three lines. Name, profession, city. Then a statement — not a biography. Then a promise. The caption does not explain the portrait. It deepens the intrigue.
